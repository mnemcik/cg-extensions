# Voice & Writing Style

A short statement of who is speaking and the overall tone you want in messages
drafted on your behalf (e.g. "direct, warm, low-ceremony; technical peers").

## Cardinal rules

The two-to-four non-negotiables that override everything below. Examples:

- Never invent facts, names, or commitments — leave a `[TODO: confirm]` instead.
- Match the language the other person wrote in.
- No corporate filler; say the thing.

## Language conventions

Which language(s) you write in, and — if more than one — when you switch.
Describe any mode-switching rule (e.g. "informal channels and same-locale
colleagues → language X; cross-team / structured / external → language Y").
Note formality differences between languages if relevant.

## Register per channel

How tone, length, and formatting change by channel and message type. Fill in the
modes you actually use; the three below are a common starting set.

### Mode A — Quick reply / short message (DMs, threads)

Length, greeting (or none), emoji use, sign-off (or none). Give a 1–2 line
example.

### Mode B — Channel announcement / broadcast ("tip of the day", FYI posts)

Structure (hook → body → call to action?), formatting (bullets, headers),
length ceiling. Give a short example skeleton.

### Mode C — Substantive message (cross-team email, decisions, technical replies)

Opening, how you structure an argument, how you present trade-offs, closing.
Note any required elements (context for outsiders, links, next steps).

## Punctuation & formatting quirks

Your idiosyncrasies: dashes vs parentheses, sentence case vs Title Case in
headings, lists vs prose, code-formatting of identifiers, capitalisation of
product names, emoji policy.

## Vocabulary signals

Words and phrases you reach for, and ones you avoid. Two short lists:

- **Use:** …
- **Avoid:** … (corporate clichés, hedging, words that aren't yours)

## Greetings & closings cheat-sheet

A quick lookup of openers and sign-offs per channel / language / formality, so
drafts don't default to a generic "Hi" / "Best regards".

## Audience awareness — strip internal context for outsiders

When a message leaves your team/org, what internal shorthand, project codenames,
or assumptions must be removed or expanded. State the rule and give an example.

## Drafting checklist

The final pass to run before a draft is ready. Examples:

- [ ] Right language for the recipient?
- [ ] Right register/mode for the channel?
- [ ] No invented facts or unconfirmed commitments?
- [ ] Greeting + closing match the cheat-sheet?
- [ ] Internal context stripped if the audience is external?

## When in doubt

What to do when the situation isn't covered above (e.g. "ask before sending",
"prefer shorter", "match the last message in the thread").
